# Act 2 - Marcia's Rebellion

Act 2 is under redesign because the campaign now takes place during the Breaching Festival and Remembrance Moon.

The old "rebellion during festivities" frame should be softened. A full street rebellion in the middle of a city-wide festival risks making the public feel foolish or tonally confused. The stronger version is that [[Marcia]] becomes the voice of a civic movement: songs, vigils, mutual aid, public mourning, food lines, and moral pressure that can later harden into resistance if the city keeps failing.

[[Marcia]] still represents genuine public rage against hunger, humiliation, biased institutions, and merchant control. She is not secretly Varro's puppet. If [[Gaius Varro]] survives Act 1 politically, his crisis creates the conditions that make her movement explosive. If the players expose or defeat him well, her movement should not vanish; it changes target and purpose.

## New Calendar Premise

- [[Day 01]] is Desnus 1, 3608.
- Day 30 is Desnus 30, 3608.
- Desnus 1 opens the Breaching Festival season.
- The first week is full of markets, music, public dinners, performers, fireworks, and caravans coming from across the country.
- Remembrance Moon turns the same public spaces toward mourning, family rites, candles, sea-offerings, and memory of the dead.
- The cult chose Desnus 30 because the black moon falls at the end of this emotionally charged month.

## Suggested Act 2 Reframe

Working title can remain **Marcia's Rebellion**, but the act should not begin as open insurrection.

Better frame:

> Marcia turns festival attention into civic conscience.

She starts with music and public anger, then becomes organizer, witness, and inconvenient moral center. Her "rebellion" is not barricades first. It is people refusing silence during Remembrance Moon.

Important: this must be playable, not just background mood. Act 2 should put the players in the middle of public choices where ignoring Marcia still changes the city.

## Act 1 Outcome Branches

Act 2 should explicitly branch from the Act 1 result.

| Act 1 Result | Public Situation | Marcia's Role | Act 2 Main Tension |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Varro good ending | Varro is exposed, weakened, arrested, exiled, or politically contained. The city avoided the obvious merchant takeover. | Marcia becomes a civic watchdog and organizer for people harmed by the crisis. She wants reparations, truth, and protection from officials who now want everyone to "move on." | The danger is backlash, cleanup, and hidden cult escalation. The city thinks the worst is over, so missing people and Remembrance Moon anomalies are easier to dismiss. |
| Varro mid ending | Varro is damaged but not gone. His allies, merchants, and officials still have leverage. | Marcia becomes a public counterweight. She is not calling for riots, but she refuses quiet compromise. | The danger is narrative war: order versus justice, stability versus truth. The players can prevent Marcia's network from being criminalized. |
| Varro bad ending | Varro gains authority or becomes the obvious stabilizing power. | Marcia becomes the face of organized resistance, but still begins with songs, aid, vigils, and witnesses before violence. | The danger is crackdown. Varro benefits if crowds panic or turn violent, so the players must decide whether to shield, steer, or distance themselves from Marcia. |

### If The Players Got The Good Varro Ending

Do not punish success by pretending Act 1 did not matter.

In the good ending version:

- food prices stabilize faster, but some families are already ruined;
- festival supply lines recover, but records still contain holes that point toward cult activity;
- officials want ceremonies of unity, not uncomfortable testimony;
- merchants blame "Varro's few bad agents" and try to avoid compensation;
- Marcia pushes for names, restitution, missing workers, and public accountability;
- the cult benefits from civic relief because everyone wants to believe the crisis is solved.

Marcia's conflict becomes:

> We won the public fight. Why are people still disappearing?

This makes her useful even after Varro falls. She is the person who refuses to let Rome confuse political victory with healing.

## Player Involvement Engine

Every Act 2 arc should involve the players in two ways: passively through unavoidable city pressure, and actively through jobs where their choices change Marcia's movement.

### Passive Pressure

These should happen even if the players do not seek Marcia out:

- Marcia's songs are heard in streets, taverns, markets, and vigils; their lyrics change based on Act 1 outcome.
- NPCs ask the players what really happened with Varro, because public memory is forming around them.
- Officials, merchants, temple staff, and poor families all treat the party as credible witnesses or dangerous meddlers.
- Remembrance Moon events put the party in crowds where names of missing people surface naturally.
- Festival logistics keep pulling them past Marcia's network: food tables, lantern crews, public stages, porters, widows, apprentices, and temple helpers.
- If they ignore the movement, rumors still evolve: Marcia is praised, mocked, blamed, censored, or protected without their input.

### Active Choices

Act 2 needs recurring decision points, not just scenes to observe.

Use these as quest engines:

| Choice | What Players Do | Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Protect a vigil or let officials disperse it | Negotiate, stand guard, expose bad orders, or walk away | Determines whether Marcia's movement is seen as lawful, dangerous, or abandoned |
| Verify names of the missing | Investigate homes, docks, shrines, and grave records | Turns grief into evidence and points toward cult activity |
| Decide who controls public aid | Back temple aid, merchant charity, Marcia's mutual aid, or official distribution | Changes who gains trust after the festival crisis |
| Stop hotheads without betraying them | Prevent sabotage, riots, or revenge attacks | Keeps Marcia's network disciplined or lets it radicalize |
| Test the "Varro is solved" narrative | Reopen records, follow missing workers, question surviving agents | Good-ending path reveals that Varro was not the only threat |
| Publicly speak or stay silent | Give testimony, refuse, or let others define events | Shapes whether the party becomes trusted civic actors or outsiders with swords |

### Make Ignoring Marcia Cost Something Concrete

If the players ignore Marcia, do not stop the plot. Let the city choose without them.

- In a good Varro ending, officials bury uncomfortable missing-person evidence under unity speeches.
- In a mid ending, Varro allies paint Marcia as the reason recovery is slow.
- In a bad ending, the first crackdown succeeds cleanly because no credible outsiders are present.
- In all versions, cult disappearances continue, and the players lose early witnesses or trust.

## Why This Works Better

- The city is already gathered, so Marcia does not need to create a crowd.
- Festival joy makes hunger and unfairness more visible, not less.
- Remembrance rites give her songs emotional legitimacy.
- Public mourning lets anger grow without requiring immediate violence.
- Varro can try to brand her movement as dangerous even when it begins as aid, testimony, and song.
- The cult can hide disappearances inside vigils, processions, masks, and grief.
- The players are not asked to care because Marcia is important; they are asked to act because public memory, missing people, and civic stability are being decided in front of them.

## Marcia's Act 2 Role

Use Marcia as:

- a performer whose festival songs become sharper each day;
- a public advocate for hungry families, missing workers, and abused poor citizens;
- a coordinator of food-sharing, shelter, and witness lists;
- a target for officials who want quiet streets during Remembrance Moon;
- a rival narrative to Varro's promise of order;
- a person who does not want bloodshed, but may be blamed for every crowd that gathers.

## Suggested Act 2 Shape

### Days 11-13 - Vigils Become Evidence

The city shifts from festival brightness into Remembrance Moon. People gather to name the dead, but some names belong to people who should not be dead yet.

Good quest fit:

- missing bodies from common graves;
- drowned shrine clues;
- first serious missing-person patterns;
- Marcia collecting names through songs and vigils.

Marcia's function: witness and amplifier.

Player-facing pressure:

- The party is asked to verify names before a public vigil.
- A family recognizes them from Act 1 and asks whether the city is safe now.
- An official asks them to advise Marcia to lower the temperature.
- If Varro fell, someone says, "Then why is my brother still missing?"

### Days 14-16 - The City Tries To Stay Festive

Markets and caravans continue, but the mood is strained. The public wants music and closure; officials want order; merchants want normal commerce; the poor want answers.

Good quest fit:

- caravan escalation;
- disappearances after vigils;
- Diego noticing patterns;
- official pressure on performers and public gatherings.

Marcia's function: organizer and pressure point.

Player-facing pressure:

- The party can guard a procession, expose a false agitator, or negotiate a legal route.
- Merchants offer charity with conditions; Marcia asks the party whether accepting it helps people or sells the story.
- Diego notices that missing-person routes overlap with remembrance processions, not with Varro's old supply routes.

### Days 17-20 - Movement, Not Riot

Marcia's supporters become a network: singers, porters, widows, apprentices, temple helpers, tavern staff, and street vendors. They are not yet an army. They are the people who know who vanished, who ate, who lied, and who paid.

Good conflict:

- officials demand permits for songs and vigils;
- Varro's allies offer controlled charity to undercut Marcia;
- Marcia refuses to let Remembrance Moon become propaganda;
- hotheads want violence, and Marcia has to decide whether to restrain them or lose them.

Marcia's function: leader under pressure.

Player-facing pressure:

- Hotheads want to turn a vigil into direct action; the party can calm, redirect, expose manipulation, or let it burn.
- If Act 1 ended well, officials ask the party to endorse a public "crisis is over" speech.
- If Act 1 ended badly, Marcia asks whether the party will stand visibly with her people when the crackdown comes.

## Practical Recommendation

Do not make Act 2 about "the rebellion begins during a festival."

Make it about:

> The festival teaches the city to gather; Remembrance Moon teaches it what it has lost; Marcia teaches it to speak.

Then, if Act 2 ends badly, the rebellion can be born from grief and crackdown rather than from random festival violence. If Act 2 goes well, Marcia's movement may remain disciplined civic resistance instead of becoming a riot.

Track what the players actively changed:

| Track | Good Direction | Bad Direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Public trust | The party is trusted as witnesses from Act 1 and protectors during Remembrance Moon | The party is treated as absent, compromised, or only useful for violence |
| Marcia's discipline | Her network stays organized around aid, testimony, and lawful pressure | Hotheads, infiltrators, or officials define the movement |

## Tone Guide

Act 2 should feel like music turning into testimony.

Keep:

- songs in public places;
- candles and names of the dead;
- caravans and temporary markets still moving;
- officials afraid of crowds;
- Marcia funny when she can be, furious when she must be;
- the cult as wrongness beneath water, memory, and missing people.

Avoid:

- a sudden generic revolution because "it is Act 2";
- Marcia ordering violence casually;
- festival crowds becoming faceless mobs too early;
- revealing the cult as the obvious answer before Diego and the Remembrance Moon clues earn it.
